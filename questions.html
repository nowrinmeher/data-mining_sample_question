Question 1: When Statistical Measures Fail (15 marks)
Scenario: A startup CEO presents these statistics about their app's daily user engagement time:
  • Mean: 45 minutes
  • Median: 8 minutes
  • Mode: 5 minutes
  • Standard deviation: 67 minutes
a) What does this data pattern tell you about user behavior? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:

The data reveals a highly right-skewed distribution with the following characteristics:

  • Extreme right skew: Mean (45 min) >> Median (8 min) indicates a few extreme outliers pulling the average up
  • Most users are light users: Mode = 5 minutes shows the most common usage pattern
  • Power user phenomenon: A small number of users spend excessive time on the app
  • High variability: Standard deviation (67 min) > Mean (45 min) indicates extremely inconsistent engagement patterns

Figure 1: Visualization of Right-Skewed User Engagement Distribution

b) Which measure would you report to investors and why? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:

Report Median (8 minutes) to investors because:

  • Honest representation: Shows that 50% of users spend ≤ 8 minutes
  • Not misleading: Mean is inflated by extreme outliers (power users or potentially bots)
  • Actionable insights: More realistic for product development and user experience decisions
  • Investor trust: Demonstrates transparency and realistic expectations
Key Business Insight: The typical user experience is 8 minutes, not 45 minutes. Product strategy should focus on engaging the majority of light users rather than relying on power users.
c) What additional investigation would you recommend? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:
  1. Power user analysis: Identify users with >60 minute sessions - are they genuine users or bots?
  2. User segmentation: Analyze engagement by demographics, acquisition channel, device type
  3. Temporal patterns: Time-of-day and day-of-week usage patterns
  4. Feature correlation: Which app features correlate with high engagement?
  5. Retention analysis: Do power users have better retention rates?
Question 2: The T-shirt Ordering Dilemma (12 marks)
Scenario: You're ordering T-shirts for a company event. Here's the size distribution:
XS: 2 people, S: 15 people, M: 25 people, L: 18 people, XL: 8 people, XXL: 2 people

Figure 2: T-shirt Size Distribution

a) If you can order only 3 different sizes, which statistical approach would you use and which sizes would you choose? (6 marks)
Expected Answer:

Approach: Use frequency analysis (mode-based approach)

Size Frequency Percentage
Medium (M) 25 35.7%
Large (L) 18 25.7%
Small (S) 15 21.4%

Selected sizes: Medium, Large, Small

Coverage: 58 out of 70 people (82.9%) get their exact size

Rationale: Minimizes the number of people with incorrect sizes while covering the highest frequency distribution.

b) The supplier offers a discount if you order just 1 size for everyone. Analyze this decision using appropriate statistical measures. (6 marks)
Expected Answer:

Statistical Analysis:

  • Mode = Medium (most frequent size)
  • Perfect fit: 25/70 = 35.7% of people
  • Reasonable fit: Including adjacent sizes (S, L) = 58/70 = 82.9%
  • Poor fit: 12/70 = 17.1% would be very uncomfortable (XS, XL, XXL)
Business Decision: Only acceptable if cost savings > 17% of people being uncomfortable.
Recommendation: Not advisable - customer satisfaction should outweigh cost savings.
Question 3: Visualization Trap (10 marks)
A marketing team creates a pie chart showing customer age distribution:
18-25: 45%, 26-35: 30%, 36-45: 15%, 46-55: 7%, 56+: 3%
Then they create a histogram of the same data and get completely different insights.

Figure 3a: Age Distribution - Pie Chart

Figure 3b: Age Distribution - Histogram

a) Why might the histogram reveal different insights than the pie chart? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:
  • Shows continuity: Histogram reveals age as continuous variable, not discrete categories
  • Distribution shape: Can identify if data is normal, skewed, or bimodal
  • Trend visualization: Shows aging customer base pattern clearly
  • Gap identification: Reveals age gaps or clusters within ranges
  • Natural ordering: Maintains age progression that pie chart loses
b) Which visualization would you trust more for business decisions and why? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:

Trust histogram more because:

  • Appropriate for data type: Age is continuous, not categorical
  • Shows natural progression: Reveals trends and patterns in customer lifecycle
  • Business insights: Can identify customer acquisition challenges, aging demographics
  • Predictive value: Better for forecasting future customer trends
  • Strategic planning: Informs marketing segmentation and targeting strategies
Business Implication: The histogram shows a young customer base that's aging, suggesting need for strategies to attract younger customers.

Question 4: The Misleading Average Problem (18 marks)
Scenario: A restaurant chain wants to analyze customer spending across 3 locations:
Location A: [12, 15, 18, 20, 22, 25, 28, 30, 35, 40] (10 customers)
Location B: [50, 55, 60, 65, 70] (5 customers)
Location C: [5, 8, 10, 12, 15, 18, 20, 25, 30, 200] (10 customers)
a) Calculate mean and median for each location. (6 marks)
Expected Answer:
Location A Calculations:
Mean = (12+15+18+20+22+25+28+30+35+40) ÷ 10 = 245 ÷ 10 = 24.5
Median = (22+25) ÷ 2 = 23.5 (middle values of sorted data)
Location B Calculations:
Mean = (50+55+60+65+70) ÷ 5 = 300 ÷ 5 = 60
Median = 60 (middle value of 5 data points)
Location C Calculations:
Mean = (5+8+10+12+15+18+20+25+30+200) ÷ 10 = 343 ÷ 10 = 34.3
Median = (15+18) ÷ 2 = 16.5 (middle values of sorted data)
Location Mean ($) Median ($) Distribution
A 24.5 23.5 Slightly right-skewed
B 60.0 60.0 Symmetric
C 34.3 16.5 Highly right-skewed

Figure 4: Restaurant Location Spending Comparison

b) The corporate office wants to rank locations by "customer value." Analyze which statistical measure leads to which ranking and explain the business implications. (8 marks)
Expected Answer:
Ranking Method 1st Place 2nd Place 3rd Place
By Mean B ($60.00) C ($34.30) A ($24.50)
By Median B ($60.00) A ($23.50) C ($16.50)

Business Implications:

  • Mean ranking misleading: Location C appears better than A due to one $200 customer
  • Median ranking more accurate: Shows Location A has consistently higher typical spending than C
  • Location B clearly premium: Both measures agree on its superior performance
  • Resource allocation impact: Using mean would incorrectly prioritize Location C over A
Critical Business Decision: Using mean would lead to wrong investment decisions. Location A should receive more resources than C based on consistent customer value.
c) Design a comprehensive analysis approach that gives the most accurate business insight. (4 marks)
Expected Answer:
  1. Primary measure: Use median as main ranking metric (robust to outliers)
  2. Variability analysis: Calculate standard deviation to understand consistency
  3. Outlier investigation: Analyze high-value customers separately (are they repeatable?)
  4. Volume consideration: Factor in customer count alongside per-customer metrics
  5. Segmentation: Analyze spending tiers (low/medium/high value customers)
Recommended Ranking: B (premium consistent), A (good consistent), C (unreliable with outlier dependency)
Question 5: Real Estate Analysis Challenge (20 marks)
Scenario: You're analyzing house prices in a neighborhood:
$180K, $190K, $200K, $210K, $220K, $230K, $240K, $250K, $260K, $1.2M
a) Calculate all statistical measures and create a comprehensive analysis report. (10 marks)
Expected Answer:
Statistical Calculations:
Data: [180, 190, 200, 210, 220, 230, 240, 250, 260, 1200] (in thousands)

Mean = (180+190+200+210+220+230+240+250+260+1200) ÷ 10 = 3180 ÷ 10 = $318K
Median = (220+230) ÷ 2 = $225K
Mode = None (all values unique)
Range = $1200K - $180K = $1020K
Standard Deviation ≈ $321K
Measure Value Interpretation
Mean $318K Heavily skewed by luxury outlier
Median $225K True middle value, more representative
Mode None All prices unique
Standard Deviation $321K Extremely high variability
Range $1,020K Massive price spread

Analysis Report:

The neighborhood shows a mixed housing market with one luxury outlier ($1.2M) significantly distorting the mean. The median ($225K) provides a more honest representation of typical home values. The extremely high standard deviation ($321K) exceeding the mean indicates exceptional variability, primarily due to the outlier property.

Figure 5: House Price Distribution with Outlier Effect

b) A real estate agent wants to advertise "average home price" in this neighborhood. What would you advise and why? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:

Advice: Use median ($225K) instead of mean ($318K)

Reasons:

  • Honest representation: Better reflects what buyers can expect to pay
  • Builds trust: Won't mislead potential buyers with inflated expectations
  • Market reality: 90% of homes are in the $180K-$260K range
  • Ethical advertising: Avoids accusations of deceptive marketing
  • Competitive advantage: Accurate pricing attracts serious buyers
Marketing Message: "Typical homes in this neighborhood are priced around $225K, with most properties ranging from $180K to $260K."
c) A buyer asks about "typical" home prices. How would you respond? (5 marks)
Expected Answer:

Professional Response:

"Typical homes in this neighborhood cost around $225,000, with most houses ranging from $180,000 to $260,000. This represents 90% of the available properties. There is also one luxury home currently listed at $1.2 million, but that's not representative of the general market here. Most buyers in your situation find homes in the $200K-$250K range that offer excellent value for this location."

Key Elements:

  • Clear typical price: $225K median
  • Range context: $180K-$260K for most homes
  • Outlier explanation: Acknowledge but contextualize luxury home
  • Buyer-focused: Relevant range for their consideration
  • Transparent: Full picture without misleading statistics
